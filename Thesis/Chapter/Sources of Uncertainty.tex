In this chapter, we will apply the framework from chapter \ref{uncertainty_estimation}
to our \textit{CIFAR10-H} dataset and investigate the following questions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item How well does the model approximate the true confusion matrix and what are
    the posterior entropies of the images?
    \item Are there subcategories with higher uncertainty than others?
    \item What is the difference in uncertainty between images that were labeled fast
    and images that were labeled slow?
    \item How does the ambiguity of an image affect the reaction time of the annotator?
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Image Ambiguity}

We used the model from chapter \ref{uncertainty_estimation} to estimate the
confusion matrix $\hTheta$. In Figure \ref{fig:confusion_matrices_comparison} we
compare the estimated confusion matrix $\hTheta$ with the true confusion matrix
$\Theta$ that we have calculated in chapter \ref{Data}. We compare the two
matrices by computing the absolute difference between the two matrices and the
relative difference between the two matrices. As one can see in the relative
differences and absolute differences in Figure
\ref{fig:confusion_matrices_comparison}, the estimated confusion matrix is very
close to the true confusion matrix\footnote{The color coding for the
relative difference is based on the logarithm of the relative difference, i.e. a
relative difference of 0.5 is colored the same as a relative difference of 2.}.\bigskip

We can perform a hypothesis test to check if the estimated confusion matrix is
significantly different from the true confusion matrix. We define the null
hypothesis as $H_0: \Theta = \hTheta$ and the alternative hypothesis as $H_1:
\Theta \neq \hTheta$. We use the vectorized versions of the matrices and define
\begin{align}
  \hat{\bdelta} := \hbvtheta - \bvtheta.
  \label{eq:delta}
\end{align}
If the null hypothesis is true, then $\E(\hat{\bdelta}) = 0$ and 
\begin{align}
  \V(\hat{\bdelta}) = \V(\hbvtheta).
  \label{eq:variance}
\end{align}
This can be used to construct the test statistic 
\begin{align}
  T = \hat{\bdelta}^T \V(\hat{\bdelta})^{-1} \hat{\bdelta} \overset{\text{a}}{\sim} \chi^2_{K\cdot(K-1)},
  \label{eq:test_statistic}
\end{align}
where $K$ is the number of latent classes\footnote{Given the high degrees of
freedom that we have, i.e. 90, we could also use a normal distribution instead
of the chi-squared distribution.}. We substitute the variance
$\V(\hat{\bdelta})$ with the estimated variance $\widehat{\V}(\hat{\bdelta})$
to calculate the p-value of the test statistic.\bigskip

Performing the test, we get a p-value of $0.859$, which means that we cannot
reject the null hypothesis, i.e. the estimated confusion matrix is
not significantly different from the true confusion matrix. This is a very
important result, because it shows that the model is able to approximate the
true confusion matrix very well, when only given the votes of the annotators.\bigskip

Using the estimated confusion matrix $\hTheta$ we can calculate the posterior
entropy of each image. The posterior entropy of image $i$ is defined as
\begin{equation}
    H_{post}^{(i)} = -\sum_{z=1}^{10} \htau^{(i)}_z \log_2(\htau^{(i)}_z), \quad i = 1, \ldots, n.
\end{equation}

As it turns out, the posterior entropy of the images is extremely low, i.e.
99.7\% of the images have a posterior entropy of $0.00000345 = 3.45\times 10^{-5}$
or lower. This means that the model is very certain about the class of the
images. A visualization of the posterior entropies is therefore not very
informative and we will not show it here. Comparing the posterior entropies of
the images $H_{post}^{(i)}$ with the prior entropies $H_{prior}^{(i)}$ shows
that we have a Pearson correlation coefficient of $0.11$ and a Spearman
correlation coefficient of $0.38$. In Figure \ref{fig:posterior_entropy_images}
we show the ten images with the highest posterior entropy, as well as their vote
distribution. At first, the images might seem unambiguous, but the vote
distribution shows that the annotators were very uncertain\footnote{The author
believes that the true categories of the images are \textit{bird - flamingo},
\textit{unknown}, \textit{deer}, \textit{deer}, \textit{deer}, \textit{dog -
although fox would probably be correct}, \textit{cat}, \textit{cat},
\textit{dog}, \textit{dog}, \textit{bird}}. Remember that the prior entropy was
high, when the votes were distributed equally among multiple categories. Our
model seems to exaggerate this property. Note that, although theoretically
possible that an image could receive equally distributed votes, it is very
unlikely in practice. But it is far more likely that there are two or three
categories that are very similar and the annotators are uncertain about the
correct category. This is well reflected in the posterior entropy of the images,
as images like the ones in Figure \ref{fig:ambiguous_image} had a high prior
entropy, but because there was a clear majority vote, the posterior entropy is
very low. In other words, in general, the posterior entropy of an image is high,
when there is no clear majority vote and almost zero when there is a clear
majority.\bigskip

However, this general interpretation is not always correct, because the
posterior entropy is also heavily influenced by the confusion matrix $\hTheta$.
In Figure \ref{fig:special_entropy} we show two images that have a very similar
vote distribution, i.e. both have $52$ total votes and $25$ votes for the
category \textit{dog}. Both images have another category with 24 votes, creating
a situation where no single category has a clear majority. But the left image
has a posterior entropy of $0.0035198$, which is far larger than the 99.7\%
quantile that we have calculated before, while the right image has a posterior
entropy of $6.2\times 10^{-15}$. Similarly, the $8^{th}$ image in Figure
\ref{fig:posterior_entropy_images} has a total of $52$ votes with $25$ votes for
the category \textit{dog} and the second most voted category is \textit{cat}
with $21$ votes, but the posterior entropy is $0.0216$.

This shows that the posterior entropy
is not only influenced by the vote distribution, but also by the confusion
matrix $\hTheta$.\bigskip

This is a very important insight, because it shows that the
posterior entropy is not only a measure of the ambiguity of the image,
but also a measure of the uncertainty of the model.

\subsection{Subcategory Ambiguity}

We will now investigate the subcategories in regards to their effect on the
confusion matrix $\hTheta$. The idea is that there might be subcategories
which generally contain more ambiguous images than others. Let $S$ be a set of
subcategories, then we define $\Theta(S)$ as the confusion matrix which was
computed using only the images from the subcategories in $S$. We can then estimate
$\Theta(S)$ and compute the difference between the confusion matrix $\hTheta$
and $\hTheta(S)$ to see, if the confusion matrix behaves differently for
subcategories.\bigskip

Due to the high number of subcategories and thus an even higher number
of possible combinations of subcategories, we will only investigate the
subcategories with a high average reaction time, because it is reasonable to assume
that those subcategories contain more ambiguous images. We specify the set $S$ as
the subcategories with an average reaction time of under $2$ seconds. $S$
contains approximately $80\%$ of the subcategories and $80\%$ of the images. The
resulting confusion matrix $\hTheta(S)$, the matrix $\hTheta$ as well as the
difference between the two matrices are shown in Figure
\ref{fig:confusion_matrices_comparison_subcategories}. The labelling uncertainty
lowered after removing the subcategories with high reaction time. This is
reflected by the fact that the relative differences outside the diagonal are
generally below 1 and furthermore, the absolute differences are generally
positive for values on the diagonal and negative for values off the diagonal.
This means that the probability for misclassification is lower in $\hTheta(S)$
than in $\hTheta$. This is an interesting result, because it shows that the
labelling uncertainty is not evenly distributed among the subcategories.\bigskip

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../figs/confusion_matrices_comparison_subcategories.png}
  \caption{Comparison of the estimated confusion matrix $\hTheta$ with the estimated confusion matrix $\hTheta(S)$, where $S$ is the set of subcategories with an average reaction time of under $2$ seconds.}
  \label{fig:confusion_matrices_comparison_subcategories}
\end{figure}

Another way to define a subset of subcategories is to use the average prior
entropy $H_{prior}^{(i)}$ of the images in the subcategory. We define the set
$S_e$ as the subcategories with an average prior entropy of under $0.325$, which
is the $80\%$ quantile of the prior entropies and contains approximately $78\%$ of
the images. The sets $S$ and $S_e$ have an overlap of $95\%$. Thus, it is
reasonable to assume that the results from the previous section also apply to
the set $S_e$. The resulting confusion matrix $\hTheta(S_e)$, the matrix
$\hTheta$ as well as the difference between the two matrices are shown in
Appendix \ref{app}, Figure
\ref{fig:confusion_matrices_comparison_subcategories_entropy}.\bigskip

Both approaches result in almost the same matrix $\hTheta(S)$ with the largest
absolute difference between $\hTheta(S)$ and $\hTheta(S_e)$ being of magnitude
$10^{-6}$.

\subsection{Reaction Time Differences}

Analysing the reaction times of the annotators can give us an insight into the
labelling uncertainty of the images. It is reasonable to assume that images
which were labeled fast are less ambiguous than images which were labeled slowly, thus
the uncertainty of the model should be lower for images which were labeled fast.
This difference in uncertainty can be useful in a practical setting, because
it can be used to assign a higher weight to images which were labeled slowly, when
training a model.\bigskip

We will now investigate the differences in uncertainty between images that were
labeled fast and images that were labeled slowly. In order to do this, we need 
to define \textit{fast} and \textit{slow}. Our goal is to define the two groups
in a way such that the difference in uncertainty is maximized. We use the following
approach:\bigskip

Let $\bm{r}$ be a set of different reaction times. We split our annotators into
two disjoint equally sized groups, $A_1$ and $A_2$ by randomly sampling from the
set of annotators without replacement. Analogous to $\bY^{(i)}$ before, we then
define $\bY_{A_1, r^+}^{(i)}$ and $\bY_{A_1,r^-}^{(i)}$ as the accumulated votes
of the annotators in group $A_1$ that labeled the image $i$ with a reaction time
of $r$ or higher and less than $r$ respectively. Analogously, we define
$\bY_{A_2, r^+}^{(i)}$ and $\bY_{A_2, r^-}^{(i)}$. Because every annotator
labeled the same number of images and had to label 20 images of every class,
both $Y_{A_1, r^+} \cup Y_{A_1, r^-}$ and $Y_{A_2, r^+} \cup Y_{A_2, r^-}$
contain the same number of images and the same distribution of true categories.
We will use group $A_1$ to find the best split of the reaction times and
then use the group $A_2$ to test if the confusion matrices of the two groups are
significantly different.\bigskip

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Estimate the confusion matrices $\hTheta(Y_{A_1, r^-})$ and $\hTheta(Y_{A_1, r^+})$
  for all $r \in \bm{r}$.
  \item Compute the test statistic $T_r$ for all $r \in \bm{r}$. The test statistic is defined as
  in equation \ref{eq:test_statistic}, but with 
  $$\hat{\bdelta} = \hbvtheta(Y_{A_1, r^-}) - \hbvtheta(Y_{A_1, r^+})$$ 
  and $$\V(\hat{\bdelta}) = \V(\hbvtheta(Y_{A_1, r^-})) + \V(\hbvtheta(Y_{A_1, r^+}))$$
  \item Find $r^* = \argmax_{r \in \bm{r}} T_r$.
  \item Compute the estimated confusion matrices $\hTheta(Y_{A_2, {r^*}^-})$ and $\hTheta(Y_{A_2, {r^*}^+})$.
  \item Test the Hyptothesis $H_0: \Theta(Y_{A_2, {r^*}^+}) = \Theta(Y_{A_2, {r^*}^-})$.
\end{enumerate}

We will now apply this approach to our dataset. As set $\bm{r}$ we use the 
$10\%, 20\%, \ldots, 90\%$ quantiles of the reaction times, i.e. 
$(1.044, 1.156, 1.256, 1.362, 1.483, 1.63 , 1.83, 2.16, 2.88)$ seconds.
Using the approach described above, we find that the best split of the reaction
times is the median with $1.483$ seconds. The estimated confusion matrices
$\hTheta(Y_{A_2, 1.483^-})$ and $\hTheta(Y_{A_2, 1.483^+})$ are shown in Figure
\ref{fig:confusion_matrices_RT_comparison}.\bigskip

The resulting p-value is indistinguishable from $0$, which means that we can
reject the null hypothesis and conclude that the confusion matrices of the two
groups are significantly different\footnote{Looking at the test statistics $T_r$
of step 2 above, we see that all splits result in a highly significant difference
between the two confusion matrices. This is interesting to note, because this means,
that the choice of $r^*$ is not too important in regards to improving the estimation of
the labelling uncertainty.}. This is a very important result, because it
shows that the reaction time of the annotators can be used to improve the estimation
of the labelling uncertainty.\bigskip

The question that arises now is, to what extent the reaction time of the annotators is
influenced by the ambiguity of the images compared to personal factors. To answer this
question, we can model the reaction time of the annotators as a function of the posterior
entropy of the images, while also controlling for personal factors.\bigskip

It seems reasonable to assume that the reaction time can be seen as a wait time
until the annotator labels the image, thus using a gamma distribution to model
the reaction time seems appropriate. However, unlike wait times, the reaction
time has a meaningful lower bound, i.e. the time it takes to see the image,
decide on the label, move the mouse to the label and click. Only after this
time, the additional time that the annotator takes to label the image can be
seen as a wait time. Therefore we will transform the reaction time by
subtracting a reasonable minimum time that it takes to label the image. Based on
the descriptive statistics of the reaction times in Figure
\ref{fig:reaction_time_gamma}, we use $0.75$ seconds, i.e. we subtract $0.75$ seconds
from the reaction time\footnote{Values that therefore become negative or zero
are set to $0.01$ seconds.}.\bigskip

In Figure \ref{fig:reaction_time_gamma} we show the distribution of the transformed
reaction times and a gamma distribution as comparison. The gamma distribution
seems to be a good fit for the transformed reaction times, which is why we can reasonably
use the following model.

We use a generalized linear mixed model with a gamma distribution, a log-link
function and a random intercept for the annotators, see
\cite{fahrmeirRegressionModelsMethods2013}. The model is defined as follows:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Structural Assumption}: The conditional mean $\mu_{ij} = \E(R_{ij}|\bgamma_{j})$ is
  linked to the linear predictor $$\eta_{ij} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 H_{post}^{(i)} + \gamma_{j}$$
  via the log-link function, i.e. $\mu_{ij} = \exp(\eta_{ij})$ or equivalently $\log(\mu_{ij}) = \eta_{ij}$.
  \item \textbf{Random Effects Assumption}: The random effects $\bgamma_{j}, j =
  1,\dots,J$ are assumed to be i.i.d. with $\bgamma_{j} \overset{i.i.d}{\sim}
  \Ncal(\bm{0}, \bm{Q})$ and $\bm{Q}$ being a positive definite matrix. It's
  certainly reasonable to assume that the average reaction time of an annotator
  can be viewed as a random approximately normal distributed deviation from the average
  reaction time of all annotators.
\end{itemize}

In conclusion, we have shown that the posterior entropy of the images has a strong relationship
with the reaction time of the annotators. We also see that a non-negligible part of
the variance in the reaction time can be explained by the annotator.
